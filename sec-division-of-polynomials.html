    Section 2.3 Division of Polynomials (ALEKS 2.3)

    We will examine two types of polynomial division: long division and synthetic division. Polynomial division can be used to factor a polynomial equation and find the zeros of a polynomial.
    🔗

    Definition 2.3.1. The Division Algorithm.

    Suppose that \(f\left( x\right)\) and \(d\left( x\right)\) are polynomials where \(d\left( x\right) \neq 0\) and the degree of \(d\left( x\right)\) is less than or equal to the degree of \(f\left( x\right)\text{.}\) Then there exists unique polynomials \(q\left( x\right)\) and \(r\left( x\right)\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f\left( x\right) =d\left( x\right) q\left( x\right) +r\left( x\right) \text{ \qquad or \qquad } \frac{f\left( x\right) }{d\left( x\right) }=q\left( x\right) +\frac{r\left( x\right) }{d\left( x\right) } \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(x) =d(x) q(x) + r(x) \text{ or } \frac{f(x)}{d(x)} = q(x) + \frac{r(x)}{d(x)} \end{equation*}
    where either the degree of \(r\left( x\right)\) is less than \(d\left( x\right)\text{,}\) or \(r\left( x\right)\) is the zero polynomial.
    🔗
    🔗

    Divide polynomials using long division.

    The polynomial \(f\left( x\right)\) is the dividend, \(d\left( x\right)\) is the divisor, \(q\left( x\right)\) is the quotient, and \(r\left( x\right)\) is the remainder.
    🔗
    🔗
    To begin the division always write the terms of the dividend and divisor in descending order with zeros as place holders for missing powers of \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 2.3.2.

    Use polynomial long division to divide \(\left( 2x^{3}-11x^{2}+13x+15\right) \div \left( 2x-5\right)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.3.3.

    Use polynomial long division to divide \(\left( -10x^{2}+2x^{3}-5\right) \div \left( x-4\right)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Divide polynomials using synthetic division.

    We can use synthetic division when the divisor is a binomial of the form \(\left( x-c\right)\) and \(c\) is a constant.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.3.4.

    \(\left( -10x^{2}+2x^{3}-5\right) \div \left( x-4\right)\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The setup:
    🔗
    Step 1:
    🔗
    Write the value of \(c\) in a box.
    🔗
    Step 2:
    🔗
    Write the coefficients of the dividend to the right of the box.
    🔗
    Step 3:
    🔗
    Skip a line and draw a horizontal line below the list of coefficients.
    🔗
    Step 4:
    🔗
    Bring down the leading coefficient in dividend and write it below line.
    🔗
    The division:
    🔗
    Step 5:
    🔗
    Multiply the value of \(c\) by the number below the line. Write the result in the next column above the line.
    🔗
    Step 6:
    🔗
    Add the numbers in the column above the line, and write result below line.
    🔗
    Repeat steps 5 and 6 until all columns have been completed.
    🔗
    The right-most number below the line is the remainder. The numbers below the line are coefficients of the quotient in order by the degree of the term.
    🔗

    Apply the Remainder and Factor Theorems.

    The value of \(f\left( c\right)\) equals the remainder we get from dividing \(f\left( x\right)\) by \(x-c\text{.}\) On the other hand, the factor theorem tells us when \(x-c\) is a factor of \(f\left( x\right)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 2.3.5. Remainder Theorem.

    If a polynomial \(f\left( x\right)\) is divided by \(x-c\text{,}\) then the remainder is \(f\left( c\right)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.3.6.

    Given \(f\left( x\right) =x^{7}-3x^{5}+2x^{2}-1\text{,}\) use the remainder theorem to evaluate \(f\left( -2\right)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 2.3.7. Factor Theorem.

    Let \(f(x)\) be a polynomial. If \(f\left( c\right) =0\text{,}\) then \(\left( x-c\right)\) is a factor of \(f\left( x\right)\text{.}\) If \(\left( x-c\right)\) is a factor of \(f\left( x\right)\text{,}\) then \(f\left( c\right) =0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.3.8.

    Use the factor theorem to determine if \((x-\sqrt{5})\) is a factor of \(P(x)\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} P\left(x\right) =x^{3}-4x^{2}-5x+20. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    REMARK: Polynomials may also be evaluated over the set of complex numbers rather than restricting \(x\) to the set of real numbers. A complex number \(a + bi\) is a zero of a polynomial \(f\left( x\right)\) if \(f\left(a+bi\right) = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 2.3.9.

    Use both the factor theorem and the remainder theorem to determine if \(8i\) is a zero of
    \begin{equation*} f\left( x\right) =2x^{3}+x^{2}+128x+64. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
