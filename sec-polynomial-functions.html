    Section 2.2 Polynomial Functions (ALEKS 2.2)

    Definition 2.2.1. Polynomial function.

    A polynomial function of degree \(n\) is a function that can be expressed as
    \begin{equation*} f\left( x\right)=a_{n}x^{n}+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+...+a_{1}x+a_{0} \end{equation*}
    where \(n\) is a non-negative integer and \(a_n \neq 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The given expression is called standard form, is the leading term, and is the constant term.
    🔗
    The domain of a polynomial is the set of all real numbers.
    🔗
    The graph of a polynomial is smooth and continuous.
    🔗
    🔗

    Identify zeros and multiplicities of zeros.

    Definition 2.2.20. Multiplicity of a zero.

    If \((x-c)^{k}\) is a factor of the polynomial \(f\left( x\right)\) and \((x-c)^{k+1}\) is not, then we say c is a zero of multiplicity k of \(f(x)\text{.}\) In other words, if a polynomial function has a factor \(\left( x-c\right)\) that appears exactly \(k\) times, then \(c\) is a zero of multiplicity \(k\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Identifying turning points.

    The turning points of a polynomial function are the points where the function changes from increasing to decreasing or vice versa. The graph of a polynomial function of degree \(n\) has at most \(n-1\) turning points.

    Theorem 2.2.30. Intermediate Value Theorem.

    Let \(f\) be a polynomial function. For \(a \lt b\text{,}\) if \(f\left( a\right)\) and \(f\left( b\right)\) have opposite signs, then \(f\) has at least one zero in the interval \(\left[ a,b\right]\text{.}\) If the signs of \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\) are the same, then the Intermediate Value Theorem is inconclusive.
    🔗
    🔗

    Applying the Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT).

    The Intermediate Value Theorem only asserts the existence of a zero when its hypothesis is satisfied. We can use the Intermediate Value Theorem repeatedly in a technique called "the bisection method" to approximate the value of a zero.
    🔗
    🔗
