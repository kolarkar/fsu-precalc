    Section 1.1 Complex Numbers (ALEKS R.6)

    One property of any given real number is that its square is always nonnegative. That is, there is no real number \(x\) such that \(x^2 = -1\text{.}\) To remedy this situation, we introduce a new number called an imaginary unit.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.1. Imaginary Unit.

    The imaginary unit, \(i\text{,}\) is defined to be a solution to the equation \(x^2 = -1\text{.}\) The solution set for the equation \(x^2=-1\) is \(\{i,-i\}\text{,}\) where \(i\) is the principal square root of \(-1\text{.}\) Thus, \(i^2=-1\) by definition, and we use the notation \(i = \sqrt{-1}\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.1.4. Complex Number.

    The standard form of a complex number is \(a+bi\text{,}\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are real numbers. The value \(a\) is called the real part and the value \(b\) is called the imaginary part.
    🔗
    🔗

    Solving polynomials over the set of complex numbers.

    The quadratic equation \(ax^2+bx+c=0\) has either one or two unique solution(s), given by
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle x=\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}. \end{equation*}
    Solving higher order polynomials involves other techniques (see Chapter 2). For instance, the expressions representing the sum and difference of two cubes can be factored as follows:
    \begin{align*} a^3 + b^3 \amp = (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2)\\ a^3 - b^3 \amp = (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2) \end{align*}
